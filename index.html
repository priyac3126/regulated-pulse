Beyond Compliance: The Intersection of AI, Inclusive UI/UX, and Digital Rights

AI & Policy · June 2026 · 8 min read

Upon a 2023 Executive Order on "safe, secure, and trustworthy" AI, a national investigation into how AI affects people with disabilities was launched (Gilbert 2-3). AI could support independence through AI-powered captioning, smart navigation tools, and accessible smart home technology.

However, it could also pose serious risks:

  • Hiring algorithms screening out applicants who are disabled
  • Education tools misjudging how students who are disabled learn
  • Public benefits systems wrongly denying support
  • Healthcare AI failing to account for disability-specific needs
  • Biometric tech that can't identify faces or gestures of people with disabilities (Gilbert 3).

The Access Board recommended transparency, inclusive datasets, and including people with disabilities at every stage of AI development to ensure equity (Gilbert 3).

The definition of disability according to the Inclusive Design Research Centre (IDRC) is aligned with the social and design models of disability: disability is socially constructed and relational. Rather than as a binary property (disabled or not) that adheres to an individual, disability is 'a mismatch between the needs of the individual and the design of the product, system or service. With this framing, disability can be experienced by anyone excluded by the design' (Costanza-Chock 53).

The table below describes how inclusive design features can directly benefit those with various disabilities and the differing roles of AI and humans in creating accessible design features (Gilbert 23; 122).

Disability Type Examples Common Inclusive Features
Physical/Mobility Paralysis, arthritis, chronic pain, limb differences Keyboard navigation, voice control, adjustable touch targets
Sensory: Vision Blindness, low vision, color blindness Screen reader support, alt text, high contrast, resizable text
Sensory: Hearing d/Deafness, hard of hearing Captions, transcripts, visual alerts, sign language interpretation
Cognitive/Learning Dyslexia, ADHD, autism, traumatic brain injury Clear layout, plain language, consistent navigation, error forgiveness
Mental Health Anxiety, PTSD, depression Reduced animations, flexible timing, calming visual design
Chronic/Invisible Long COVID, epilepsy, chronic fatigue, migraines Customizable contrast, reduced motion settings, flexible formats
Temporary or Situational Broken arm, noisy environment, multitasking Voice input, captions, one-handed use, adaptable interface design

A recommended approach to creating accessible design is for design teams to engage in community co-design, involving disabled users in the design process. This has been shown to result in "45% better usability scores and 38% fewer accessibility violations compared to traditional design-then-test approaches" (Teeters 156-157).

An interesting example of an AI-integrated assistive technology that works with community members and people with disabilities would be the Be My Eyes app. The "AI feature enables blind or low vision users to take photos and receive detailed text descriptions, offering capabilities beyond basic object identification, including text recognition, mood interpretation, and image quality feedback with suggestions for retaking unclear photos. If the AI-generated description proves inadequate, users can request additional information or connect with a human volunteer for further assistance" (Gilbert 183).

AI is also becoming a useful partner in UX research, being able to flag accessibility violations, track user flows, and provide automated insights. However, human researchers currently still remain essential in addressing the subtle nuances of user intent, emotions, and cultural context– all important in designing accessible UI/UX design.

One way organizations can create more inclusive web design is to maintain an accessibility statement that describes their current progress and provides a way for users to report issues. An Equally Alternative Access Plan is also an effective strategy as it creates alternative ways website information and its offered tasks can be accessed by those with disabilities.

What AI Can Do (Fast, Scalable, But Limited) What Humans Must Do (Ethical and Accountable)
Detect Web Content Accessibility Guideline violations (e.g., low contrast, missing alt text) Understand how barriers affect people differently based on race, gender, disability, class, etc.
Generate accessible formats (captions, transcripts, alt text) Ensure those formats reflect real-world context and lived experience
Process thousands of pages, code, or apps quickly Identify patterns of exclusion that data alone won't show
Apply standards evenly without bias (in theory only) Recognize how "neutral" standards can still exclude marginalized voices
Translate content into multiple formats automatically Tailor accessibility for cultural, linguistic, and regional needs
Flag technical gaps at scale Address systematic gaps that AI isn't trained to see (e.g., who is left out entirely)

Designing inclusively means designing for human dignity. It means creating a world that decreases disability as it is defined by the social and design model (Gilbert 6). It decreases systemic barriers and friction those with disabilities face between their identity as users and their environments.

Furthermore, over 1 billion people live with a disability (Gilbert 8). That is $490 billion in disposable income of working-age adults. Increasingly, accessibility is a requirement to business success, as consumers have increasingly valued accessibility and inclusion in who they buy from.

Inclusive Design for a Digital World provides some fast facts on this:

  • Nearly 70% of consumers say a brand's diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) values influence where they shop.
  • 45% say they'll even pay more for products from brands that reflect those values.
  • Almost 90% of digital professionals say that accessibility investments have improved overall user experience for all users (Gilbert 8).

In addition to this, a 2024 study from MIT's Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory "found that 73% of breakthrough interface innovations originated from accessibility requirements before being adopted by mainstream users" (Teeters 155). Two notable examples are email and voice-to-speech technology that were born out of the disabled community's need for alternative communication methods.

Jamaal Davis, an Inclusive UX Expert, also uses these two words to get the attention of companies intending to build fast and exclude many: liability and revenue (Teeters 164; 166).

Title III of the American Disabilities Act (1990) requires that businesses open to the public, including websites, be accessible to those with disabilities ("Americans").

Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) and Sections 504 and 508 of The Rehabilitation Act of 1973 extend this a step further– taking away federal funding from organizations that are not accessible.

For instance, this section of the ACA ensures not just a person's disability status, but attributes such as race, color, national origin, and sex are protected from discrimination from health programs or activity that receive any funding from the Department of Health and Human Services ("Section 1557").

The Rehabilitation Act's Sections address this concern in federal agencies to ensure the Information and Communication Technology (ICT) used by employees and members of the public are accessible ("Understanding"). It also prohibits discrimination against those with disabilities in these areas: "Programs and activities receiving federal financial assistance or conducted by executive agencies or the U.S. Postal Service" ("Understanding").

Overall, the relationship between accessible UI/UX design and artificial intelligence is one of nuance. AI increasingly is becoming a co-pilot in UX research, an integral tool for designers. However, designers must build UI/UX design with both the importance of accessibility and the limitations and risks of artificial intelligence in mind. Only then can we build an inclusive society towards those that are able-bodied and disabled alike.

One of the goals of user-interface design is "to reduce the user's cognitive load to a minimum" (Costanza-Chock 55). An important point Design Justice makes is that user-interface decisions will result in some users having the privilege of having a lower cognitive load than others and that this is not inherently wrong (Costanza-Chock 55). Default language settings are one example of this that is common and acceptable (Costanza-Chock 55).

Another example of this concept is in two versions of a university admissions portal's race/ethnicity selection process (Costanza-Chock 58). In one version, the default race/ethnicity is set to White, Non-Hispanic, the most common race/ethnicity group that will create a personal profile on this site (Costanza-Chock 58). On the other version, there is no default selected (Costanza-Chock 58). The majority of white users will have a smoother experience under Version A, as it requires less clicks; however, this version produces a slightly more time and click consuming (increased cognitive load) experience for people of color– perhaps along the lines of a microaggression.

The same essential principle of user-interface design being implemented in these two frequently encountered scenarios for UI/UX designers has a different ethical and sociotechnical component, one that would be perceived differently by different designers and users. This highlights the nuances hidden behind lines of code, or, rather, with the advancement of AI, the more technical aspect this job is known for.

Where the answers are not clear-cut is where designers must step in, and bring the community to the table. UI/UX design matters in a world that is ever-so-reliant on technology. It is integral to do the work to make sure designs are inclusive and accessible not just to obey the law, but to stay true to the governing principle of what it means to be a human– to have empathy and a moral compass.

Where a corporation sees a shortcut in seeing a product to market is where Inclusive UX Experts such as Davis shake their head and say the magic words: liability and revenue.

A Giant Leap: Trust and Oversight in Healthcare AI

Book Review · June 2026 · 5 min read

Published Feb 2026

"Doctors used to be empathic,” he told me, “but the system has sucked the soul out of everybody."

— A Giant Leap, page 186

The slow erosion of the patient-doctor relationship in the United States the past few decades has been caused by a mixture of electronic health record (EHR) implementation, the shortage of healthcare providers, and the nature of how our healthcare system is funded– being billed per service. The ideal of having a lifelong relationship with your primary care provider is not the reality most of us share.

This erosion has reduced trust in this relationship which serves two distinct purposes as artificial intelligence technologies are implemented across healthcare– it makes it easier for patients to trust in newly implemented AI tools due to the lower level of trust in the current system and harder because they may question the tools’ accuracy and the motives behind institutional adoption (Wachter 50).

Patients may think AI, as it is used in other sectors, is a way to decrease labor costs and increase margins.

But AI can do, and is doing, both, all while performing at higher levels of accuracy than providers in some instances. For instance, when faced with the “famously vexing cases” of The New England Journal of Medicine, generative AI was on par with the best faculty diagnosticians (Wachter xix).

The doctor-author remarks on this insight with the humorous remark: “Was this how it felt to be a skilled portrait painter in the late nineteenth century, when photography emerged as a much faster and cheaper way to capture people’s likenesses?”

Although we aren’t quite there yet with healthcare AI, in our lifetimes, it is highly probable that the abilities of artificial intelligence will transform the healthcare landscape.

Besides, it’s not like humans are void of making millions of serious medical mistakes a year. As Dr. Wachter points out, “Nearly one million Americans are severely harmed or killed by medical mistakes every year” (xxi). On top of that, one-of-three dollars spent in healthcare are spent on administrative costs– suddenly, incorporating AI to ease administrative costs, and use in clinical practice, as a doctor-in-the-loop is utilized, doesn't seem like such a bad idea.

Actually, it seems like the shake-up that our healthcare system desperately needs.

But, what does the regulatory landscape look like for these technologies that advance every day?

As it's called in Silicon Valley slang, let’s double click on that. As the Trump administration scales back on the federal government's regulatory arm, FDA commissioner Rob Califf argues “The agency needs more resources to carry out its functions, particularly as new AI tools become ubiquitous features of clinical care” (Wachter 234). Healthcare regulation has always walked a tight-rope: too much regulation and you stifle innovation, too little and you endanger lives. As we give technology greater responsibilities, we must reckon with the risks this may bring.

Generative AI can hallucinate one minute and provide flawless specified output the next, and then act like it “forgot” the specificities of prior conversations the next. Anyone that has played around with Gen AI enough can tell you that much. So, when it comes to the lives of ourselves and our loved ones, how does the dance between biomedical ethics and technological healthcare innovation go? Is it a graceful one?

As long as there’s a human in the loop, many of us would say yes, given current technological capabilities and for the foreseeable future.

But, there’s a few issues with that; namely, humans are not very good at being vigilant against reliable technologies and de-skilling would likely occur as they become more dependent on these technologies.

One poignant example lies in the 2009 Air France Flight 447 crash off the coast of Brazil. As the plane’s airspeed sensors failed, the pilot and copilot were on their own to guide the plane through a strong gust of wind. An event that wouldn't have typically been anything special became the scene of a fatal incident, as 228 people plunged into the Atlantic Ocean.

In the words of the late aviation journalist William Langewiesche, “We are locked into a spiral in which poor human performance begets automation, which worsens human performance, which begets increasing automation” (Wachter 87).

But before you write off this troubling incident as the crime of two bloody hands, and not of human nature, take a look at this study.

“In a dramatic 1998 study, research psychologist Kathleen Mosier placed twenty-one experienced commercial pilots in a flight simulator. During the flight, the pilots were shown a warning light pointing to a potential fire in one of the engines. The twist was that several other indicators showed no signs of a fire, making it overwhelmingly likely that this was a false alarm. Nevertheless, all twenty-one of the pilots shown the warning light decided to shut down the engine, a dangerous move. In subsequent interviews, two-thirds of the pilots reported having seen at least one other indicator confirming the fire (several recalled seeing more than one), despite the fact that there were no such cues, a phenomenon Mosier dubbed “phantom memory.” Our trust in technology can become so powerful that we ignore our own senses, creating a special problem for the human in the loop” (Wachter 88).

Hard to read, right? Especially considering this aspect of human psychology likely has not dramatically shifted since the late 90s.

Another reason why the human-in-the-loop idea may not be as strong as we think: sometimes the human makes it worse, particularly in clinical diagnostic reasoning.

In a study run by Stanford informatics expert Jonathan Chen, “Physicians were given cases to review; half (the control group) were allowed to use their usual resources, and half were given access to GPT-4. The control group ended up with a score of 74 percent on a measure of diagnostic reasoning, whereas the group with access to GPT-4 scored 76 percent– a trivial improvement. The shocker was that when GPT-4 was given the cases to review by itself, without any input from the physicians, it scored 92 percent” (Wachter 90).

There are a few alternative solutions to keeping a more direct human-AI partnership while keeping the nature of us in mind, including having the doctor to be the first to weigh in and having AI systems periodically give false diagnoses, chart summaries, or recommendations (Wachter 91). However, this latter strategy puts an essential glass ceiling on healthcare AI capabilities. Isn’t the whole point of the man-machine collaboration to advance beyond capabilities man can achieve alone, to the furthest achievable point to benefit humanity?

There is no right answer to many of these kinds of questions when we involve artificial intelligence. But, as AI revolutionizes healthcare, how do we train the next generation of doctors?

They will need to be trained with less rote memorization and a greater skillset in how to use AI tools wisely. Physicians spend 45-65% of their work day staring at a screen, and as AI advances, they will be better able to be caring towards patients they see for 10-15 minute windows. The pace of AI advancement has surpassed that of medical schools curriculum, and something more important– federal regulation.

When Dr. Wacher met with the United States’ chief health AI official in 2024, a newly established position itself, he asked a question that is quite revealing of the healthcare AI federal regulatory landscape.

“‘If we throw a wrapper on GPT-4 and it gives me diagnostic advice when I’m seeing a patient– but I, as a physician, still need to sign off at the end– where does that live in the regulatory framework?”’

His response: “‘Dunno yet’” (Wachter 240).

Although A Giant Leap was released in February of 2026, healthcare AI regulation is still a gray area in many ways, and will continue to be as AI grows in a direction that will force citizens and lawmakers alike to think about technology ethics in this important sector.

As former national coordinator for health IT David Blumenthal and Google’s chief of regulatory affairs Bakul Patel argued in writing, “How can the government regulate a single device that could be applied to any conceivable clinical problem, whose knowledge base dwarfs any human’s and changes constantly, whose methods of reaching conclusions are beyond the comprehension even of its creators, and whose computing capacity grows by leaps and bounds?” (Wachter 240-241).

Situated in the center of this debate is the Coalition for Health AI (CHAI) that was founded in 2021 and has over three thousand member organizations including tech giants, academic hospitals, AI start-ups, and government agencies. Most notably, CHAI runs a national network of assurance labs primarily at academic health systems that test and validate healthcare AI tools and provides research-based guidance to promote responsible AI use (Wachter 243). It’s an important organization that encourages transparency and encourages safe AI use in a competitive, yet incredibly important industry: healthcare.

As the AI race moves full steam ahead, it is organizations like CHAI that help fill a regulatory void that the government could not. CHAI makes the healthcare AI industry one step ahead because of the intense and intentional collaboration it fosters, and gives me hope despite the risks and unpredictable issues that come with a revolution in healthcare.

As Mayo Clinic CEO and author Dr. Wachter remarked, in this space, “The risks of going too slowly exceed the risks of going too fast” (258). AI will disrupt the healthcare industry– but not before lawyers, consultants, and accountants feel the heat (Wachter 262).

Healthcare’s primary function is to maximize human health, and if wise AI implementation can assist with that, then let it be– Epic’s monopolistic and anticompetitive practices be damned (Wachter 214-222).

  • Wachter, Robert. A Giant Leap: How AI Is Transforming Healthcare and What That Means for Our Future. Portfolio / Penguin, an Imprint of Penguin Random House LLC, 2026.

The Realities of Chronic Illness

Personal · June 2026 · 6 min read

"When, I wondered, had I last yearned for something other than simply feeling better?"

— The Invisible Kingdom, page 92

It was Monday, September 9, 2024. My eyes opened, natural sunlight from our small dorm windows greeting me. But, before I could walk down the steps of my bunk bed and get ready for class, I had to confront the bodily sensations deluding me out of the healthy privilege I had grown used to for the past nineteen years. I had just gotten a shaded butterfly tattoo for my nineteenth birthday and was still learning to embrace being a college freshman- the communal bathrooms, sharing a dorm with someone, and learning to navigate the enormous University of Georgia campus. I would also soon learn how to navigate the American healthcare system in the throes of chronic pain for the next four months of my life, without answers for why I was in debilitating pain, what could mitigate it, and how this would affect my academic career. It was a rough period, no doubt, but, somehow I survived to tell the tale- moreover, I survived to create a major and professional aspirations stemming from those experiences. Making a major and planning to acquire over a decade of post-secondary education is an arduous journey, but nothing compares to waking up for the next 123 days in the worst pain of your life, an invisible illness no test or doctor can explain.

For those suffering from chronic pain, a diagnosis is more than a descriptive phrase on an electronic health record, a set of code words hard to pronounce that clinical professionals break down to a patient's level. It's a label that makes us feel seen, understood, and gives us hope. Hope of a cure, and for someone facing our symptoms in the future to get definitive answers, more than that, the answers we deserve. In reading The Invisible Kingdom, I came across a dance of words in which I felt that my experience was shared by others, people I didn't know, but I understood and they understood me in a way that those closest to us may never, despite their empathetic attempts. I couldn't imagine a better quote to begin this article (O'Rourke 17).

"But this is not a story of recovery or the overcoming of a disease; the focus is not on my being a survivor, or getting “better,” as my objective turned out to be learning to live with uncertainty and incapacity. Without data or answers, I had to make room for a reality that included my near-total lack of control. I still don't have definitive answers. But gradually, peeling away uncertainty layer by layer, I arrived at what feels, to me at least, like a truth I can live with."

Autoimmune diseases affect over 15 million Americans (Abend 2), and they are rising at near epidemic rates according to scientists ("Latest Policy" 3). However, the Autoimmune Association estimates that as many as 50 million could be affected due to the dramatic changes in environment and lifestyle that have occurred since 1991. Antinuclear antibodies (ANA), commonly found in those suffering from autoimmune issues, have tripled in prevalence in adolescents since then, and women account for 80% of autoimmune patients ("Autoimmune" 1). When accounting for women of color, the health disparities enlarge, as Black and Hispanic women are diagnosed with lupus, a common autoimmune condition, at nearly three times the rate of non-Hispanic white women, and have a mortality rate of that multiple as them as well ("Latest Policy" 4). When we account for how women of color are underrepresented in clinical trials, and often have greater barriers in accessing the healthcare essential to being diagnosed, the disparities widen further. Furthermore, according to the Autoimmune Association, "It takes an average of three years (and four doctors) for a sufferer to be given a diagnosis of an auto-immune disease," a level of effort, time, and resources that those of marginalized communities often do not have. In addition to that, reliable diagnostic tools are still scarce for the over 100 autoimmune diseases and there is no cure for them, making chronic pain the reality of millions. Moreover, one Harvard researcher points out that "Medical science's understanding of auto-immunity lags a decade behind its understanding of cancer," a disease still only partially understood (O'Rourke 45).

Until medicine catches up to the needs of those suffering from autoimmune issues, whether they know it or not, it pays to understand the plight of these "invisible" people. It is only by bringing their stories to the light can we increase public awareness, and, therefore, federal funding and policy reform to better face the autoimmune disease epidemic. For instance, over one billion was allocated by the National Institutes of Health to autoimmune disease research ("Report" 1), conditions that 23.5-50 million Americans suffer from. Meanwhile, 8.1 billion was allocated to cancer research ("Report" 1) (over two million projected new cases and ~611k projected deaths for 2024) (Siegel 12) and over three billion to HIV/AIDS research ("Report" 1) (over 1.2 million Americans had HIV in 2024, AIDS refers to the latest HIV infection stage) ("CDC" 1). In telling my story, with the courage that reading O' Rourke's health testimony has brought me, I hope to make these numbers feel more real, more connected to the stories of our loved ones and communities, making us feel more passionately about how to reduce the pain that our fellow Americans face- if only there were more resources to do this literal life or death work.

When I briefly tell my story to new friends or those that ask why I feel so passionately about pursuing healthcare technology law, they are often surprised. My story sounds like a living nightmare for any nineteen-year-old far from home to face, especially as I did not walk into university with a strong peer network or access to a car- and it was. There were numerous doctors' appointments, primarily with my primary care physician at the University Health Center, but also with neurologists, physical therapists, ear, nose, and throat doctors, and as a final suggestion of my primary care physician, a massage therapist. Coming from an upper middle class background and going to a university that had many of these services on-campus, I came from a position of privilege. But illness discriminates against no one. At the end of a four month journey, I was given "the talk" by my primary care physician that we might never know what triggered my sudden health decline, and we won't know how long it will last if it will ever end, or know of a cure. Lucky for me though, a commonly known neuropathic pain relieving medication gave me a 97% reduction in symptoms: amitriptyline (Moore 1). A simple Google search will lead you to seeing that it is most commonly used as an anti-depressant ("Amitriptyline" 1)- which leads me to my next point.

It is estimated that up to one-third of those with a serious medical condition will have symptoms of depression ("Coping" 1). I was one of them. To anyone suffering from unresolved chronic health issues, it's a given that it will take a mental toll on you. How could it not? I was wasting away "the best years of my life" in and out of medical institutions, not getting answers and spending thousands in the process. My debilitating headaches were causing me to face declined cognitive function- it was harder to focus and my memory got worse. The same Introduction to Psychology exam I would have spent a few days before preparing, I now had to spend 4-6 weeks to do just as well. Combined with the fact that I had to withdraw from Introduction to Biology and its lab because I physically couldn't complete the course, all while wanting to be an Anesthesiologist's Assistant further complicated matters.

As I got no answers from months of seeking treatment, I had to make peace with the reality that I may never be "normal" again. Having the same intelligence that put me in the top 5% of my graduating class academically may never be in reach again. The reason why I was at the University of Georgia was taken from me and I spent the next few months trying to figure out what academic and professional path I would pursue in its absence. I stumbled upon law because it was the only academic and professional sector left. I had realized math and introductory science courses were not particularly interesting and I always excelled at English. Toss in an extended, eye-opening experience in the American healthcare system, and an interest in artificial intelligence, and we've got the building blocks to a 17-page major curriculum for Healthcare Technology Law and Regulation through UGA's Interdisciplinary Studies program. It is, as my former freshman-year roommate said, excellent material for graduate school personal statements. I would add, an extremely costly one.

The only reason I was able to survive this period in my life was not due to unwilting optimism in regards to my poor health, but because of two important things in my life: God and my loved ones. When I caught myself daydreaming about the afterlife, I reminded myself that God's Plan is intentional and will, one day, make sense- even if you wake up in the worst pain of your life for 123 consecutive days in a row. O' Rourke's statement, "But I had not anticipated what should have been my biggest fear of all: that I would lose hope" could not ring more true, for as long as we have hope, we can endure. The never-ending dance between severe health issues and balancing being a normal college freshman was not one of grace. Some days were better than others, but, till this day, over 1.5 years later, not a day passes where I can fully forget what it is that afflicts me. I don't know how my neck accumulated severe nerve damage- I never got injured. The closest guess anyone has is that carrying my backpack around campus put increased pressure on my back and could have aggravated the issue. It's not a particularly logically strong conclusion but chronic health issues rarely are. People who suffer from them rarely get a "heads-up" from their doctor that this may be in their cards or how to avoid it. Autoimmune diseases stem from one-third genetic and two-thirds environmental causes, but as O' Rourke points out, many like her try supplement, food, therapy, and other lifestyle modification after another to no avail. As I mentioned earlier, health issues do not discriminate so as a well-off educated middle-aged married straight white woman, she often threw money, time, and effort essentially at a brick wall.

In her own words:
"‘It's bearable, and yet I cannot bear it," wrote Alphonse Daudet of living with syphilis. I had revised his line in my head: "It is unbearable- and yet I bear it." I survived, but it was the nature of the disease to rob me of myself."

- O' Rourke, pg. 261

As I write this article for The Regulated Pulse, I am reminded of my former reality. I doubt if anyone can be more passionate about pursuing a certain academic and career track than me as I really do believe I did not go through Earth's version of Hell to turn around and not work towards advancing healthcare for Americans for the rest of my life- through policy and advocacy work as well as through the cases I may encounter as a practicing attorney. I can't write this piece without mentioning the elephant in the room when it comes to the struggles of those with serious health issues: the lack of clinical empathy we often face. There were several times during the throes of my health challenges I would get quite emotional in the doctor's office. Of course, it was not my intention to elicit Kleenexes being handed to me while I sat on a stiff patient's table but to go through pain for an extended period of time like that and not know why can be extremely frustrating. There were multiple times my primary care physician would suggest I seek therapy, and wondered how my parents were faring as I went through this while living three-hours away. And therapy I did go to. Once a clinical pain psychologist was employed by the University Health Center, I knew it was high-time to seek mental health help. Although my friends were critical to me feeling heard and understood, it was nice being able to dump your problems on a middle-aged white lady that made empathetic faces as you ranted about why nearly your whole college career has been a complete sham.

This quote summarizes the source of my frustrations quite poetically:
" I don't believe I will get better," Daudet wrote, "... yet I always behave as if my damned pains were going to disappear by tomorrow morning."

But, alas, life isn't so bad anymore, and for that I am quite grateful. My beloved amitriptyline's price with insurance did increase by 2,252.94% last month, but, fortunately, that is still $20 for a month's supply. I think I can spare a 20 to function normally (for the most part; I still deal with the occasional tics, convulsions, and breathing problems that also began at the same time my headaches did, but, you know, life's not fair). I have thought about what I would do if it suddenly jumped to, let's say, $200 or $400 and the thing is, like many Americans, I would just have to find the money. It is high time the United States implements a nationalized single-payer healthcare system- costs have never been higher, and as I've experienced, financial stress only adds to your existing health issues. Being referred to one hospital for my MRI instead of a local clinic meant paying $1,600 versus $400 with insurance. Americans deserve better, or rather the bare minimum as most other rich democratic nations have shown.

Interdisciplinary Studies: Healthcare Technology Law & Regulation

University of Georgia · Franklin College of Arts and Sciences · A.B. Interdisciplinary Studies

Designed for students at the intersection of public health, technology law, and public policy, this major investigates the regulatory frameworks and ethical foundations required to protect society's most vulnerable. Requiring 24 hours of major coursework, the curriculum examines how marginalized groups such as the sick, disabled, elderly, immigrants, and those suffering from mental health and substance abuse issues interact with the healthcare system. It provides specialized training in healthcare management, American law, and how ethics interplay with law, business, and medicine.

Required Core Courses

All students must complete these courses:

  • SOCI 3110: Sociology of Healthcare OR SOCI 3100/H: Medical Sociology
  • HPAM 3600: Introduction to Health Policy
  • One course in Artificial Intelligence (e.g., ARTI 2130, ARTI 2550, MIST 5400)
  • INTS 4990R: Undergraduate Research Thesis or Final Project

About Me

After navigating an unexpected chronic illness journey during my freshman year, I experienced firsthand the systemic fractures within the American healthcare system.

This exposure shifted my focus from clinical pre-health tracks toward a deeper calling: reshaping the legal, regulatory, and business frameworks that dictate how care is delivered.

This inspired me to design an Interdisciplinary Studies major at the University of Georgia, focusing on Healthcare Technology Law and Regulation. I will be graduating from this program next spring.

My focus sits at the intersection of emerging AI, compliance frameworks, and inclusive UI/UX design.

I am dedicated to moving beyond mere legal compliance to advocate for patient welfare and advance accessible technology rights.

As an aspiring healthcare attorney, my long-term vision is to eventually transition into academia as a professor specializing in healthcare AI and technology law.
